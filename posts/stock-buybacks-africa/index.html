Stock Buybacks in Africa: What Corporate Repurchases Mean for Investors

April 23, 2026 · 3 min

Introduction#

African corporations have begun engaging in stock buyback programs in 2026 where companies repurchase their own shares from the market reducing the total number of shares outstanding while concentrating ownership among remaining shareholders.

This practice which is more common in developed markets has been less prevalent in Africa historically but growing cash generation by successful African companies is enabling more buyback programs that have significant implications for shareholders.

How Stock Buybacks Work#

A corporation with excess cash decides to repurchase some of its shares from the open market. The company pays whatever the current market price is for the shares it purchases. The repurchased shares are held as treasury stock or retired reducing the total number of shares outstanding.

Since company earnings remain the same but the number of shares has decreased the earnings per share metric increases mechanically even if the company’s underlying profitability has not improved.

Buybacks in African Markets#

Several of the largest African listed companies have announced buyback programs in 2026. Safaricom announced a KSH 20 billion buyback of its own shares. MTN Nigeria has implemented buyback programs. Standard Bank has repurchased shares multiple times. First National Bank in South Africa has active buyback programs.

These programs signal management confidence that the company’s shares are undervalued at current prices and that deploying capital to repurchase shares generates better returns for remaining shareholders than alternative uses of capital.

Benefits of Buybacks for Shareholders#

Increased earnings per share from the mechanical effect of fewer shares means that remaining shareholders own a larger fraction of company earnings. If a company with 1 billion shares earns 1 billion dollars has 1 dollar of earnings per share after buying back 100 million shares and keeping earnings constant the remaining shares have 1.10 dollars of earnings per share.

Buybacks also provide management with a flexible way to return capital to shareholders while maintaining dividend payments. Dividends paid every quarter create a fixed commitment while buybacks can be increased or stopped based on cash generation and company circumstances.

Tax efficiency in some jurisdictions makes buybacks more tax efficient than dividends from the shareholder perspective though this varies by jurisdiction.

Criticisms of Buyback Programs#

Buybacks can signal that management lacks better investment opportunities and is returning cash to shareholders rather than investing in the business. Excessive buyback programs can reduce capital available for research development and capacity expansion that might benefit long term value creation.

Buybacks at inflated prices can destroy shareholder value if the company repurchases shares at prices that later prove to be excessive relative to actual company value.

Conclusion#

Stock buyback programs in African corporations are becoming more common as successful companies accumulate excess cash. For shareholders buybacks represent an alternative to dividends for returning capital though they have different implications for company finances and long term value creation. Investors should monitor buyback programs critically evaluating whether share repurchases are appropriate uses of company capital or whether alternative deployments might create greater long term value.
